What?
This is a story about taste, really. It's not about C++ until the ending. This is introspection about how I developed an opinion on programming languages. It's motivated by a realization: I enjoy watching CPPCon talks but used to think I hated C++.
I'll probably include some tips on learning C++ at the end -- in a way that avoids the pitfalls that led me to think I hated it. If you just want that part of this, which is probably the better part anyway, skip to it here.
The Origin Story
I was lucky with coding: since my parents work in IT, when I expressed an interest in MS LOGO and flowcharts as a fifth grader, they were quick to get me to learn Scratch, and even today, understand a lot about what I do.
I moved to the US in high school and coming from England meant that I had no clue how grades worked. In England, you can just track yourself along national curricular levels and I was content with my standing. American grades, with percentage breakdowns and the GPA made no sense. This motivated me to try to make an app (that I haven't made still). So I learned Java, because I had an Android.
Then my dad found codecademy.com and that opened Pandora's box. I learned JS, then PHP, then Python, then Ruby. I didn't really build too much in these languages yet, but I loved absorbing new syntax and learning random tricks and tools.
After this, in talking to my dad's colleague about the differences between computer science, computer engineering, and software engineering, I realized that I liked computer science the most. He also gave me a book on Prolog which I hungrily absorbed. But Prolog was the start of something different: since it wasn't like the other languages that led me to just want to build stuff. It led me to wonder what was and wasn't programming. There was another side-effect: my dad's colleague told me that he thought the Prolog book would be better than the Lisp book. This led me to learn Lisp once I got bored of Prolog (mostly since my family tree encoding wouldn't work since I think I had some circular definition of nieces and nephews -- I was (am) a bad coder). Lisp, however, was probably my first real love in programming languages. It let me add syntax as I pleased, which was something I missed in other languages. I loved that my abstractions weren't second to that language's.
After this, I pretty much did Java and Python coding with occassional clojure projects. My AP CS teacher's use of Git led me to make my derpspace as a set of eclipse projects. I wandered into Linux around this time and got super distracted trying to understand Gtk and compiz's C++ code (not that I understood anything).
Into Undergrad
In undergrad, since I learned about linked-lists as cons-cells in Lisp, and a friend taught me what a BST is in high school, I found the first half of data structures really easy. I used my spare time to build Bene-fish. So I knew some web-dev. I was also a Rich Hickey fan and so tried to build a persistent stack in C++. I knew nothing about what I was trying to do and how to allocate memory.
There's a ton of other projects I could mention, but it's about time I get to talking about C++ instead of dawdling on about Haskell, Rust, and other random stuff I learned. Really, the type theory points are covered in my rant about that not being taught.
About C++
Once I understood memory, confident in my C coding, I thought I understood how painful memory management should be. Little did I know that C over does it. That manually calling free (delete) is a thing of 10 years ago in C++ and has never been a thing in Rust. Not even with a garbage collector.
C++ scared me when I thought memory management was only garbage collection or C-style malloc and free. Object-orientation looks very complicated when you think you have to allocate and deallocate by hand. It's an unfortunate thing to learn. I tried to learn Rust's memory system based on C++, reasoning that I could at least read much of C++, so it'd make understanding Rust better.
